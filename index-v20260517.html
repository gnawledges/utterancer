Stages: VAD splits the recording at acoustic silences; the chapteriser recursively bisects on the longest pauses to produce 5–10 minute chunks; ASR runs per chapter (large enough for context, short enough to fit comfortably); the chapter transcripts are stitched and the full recording is forced-aligned for word-level timestamps; the human annotator drives the final segmentation and inline transcription through the web interface code lives in the gnawledges/utterancer repository.

Recordings

Both recordings are public domain materials that are longer than 10 minutes, but vary across several different dimensions: speaker gender, language, prosodic style, etc. As can be heard from the Obama sample ("despite [pause] the real and troubling [pause] areas where freedom appears in retreat"), the Obama recording provides plenty of spots where the utterance contains several long pauses. The Ekka sample is much more evenly paced in the genre of an audiobook recording.

Chapterisation

As can be seen from the fragment count (Obama = 795; Ekka = 587), the VAD stage that relies only on acoustics produces many utterance fragments that we have to try and merge. The between- and within-utterance pause durations don't cleanly separate into a bimodal distribution. That said it is more likely for the longest of the pauses to be between utterances than within. Based on this tendency, we recursively bisect the longest chapters at their longest pause until every chapter is under 10 minutes.

Note we're not too worried about uneven breaks: given the 8.4 hour advertised context there's no issue of load balancing (see recording-initial/-final chapters of Obama being 3.6 minutes or Ekka being 1.5 minutes). There is a merge pass to combine any chapter less than a minute back with its shortest neighbour. Even left at 1.5 minutes, however, this still sets up the ASR with enough context than the shortest fragments coming out of a bottom-up approach (e.g. ~5-10 second fragments submitted to ASR without surrounding context). Further, from a cost perspective, the medium-length chapters allow for fewer job submissions, reducing the instruction prompt repetition (can add up when the prompt is massive!), also allowing for more specificity within each prompt.

Transcription with punctuation

The prompts are language-specific and specify script-specific punctuation, e.g. Latin . vs. Devanagari ।, which we'll use as hints during the merge step. As the English text transcript already shows with Mr. President., the punctuation should only be taken as hints so the pause durations are very still relevant! Although this is a hint that the prompt should demonstrate verbalisations such as Mr. -> Mister because the forced alignment with work better on verbalised forms.

Word-level timestamps with forced alignment

For forced alignment, we use the fairseq MMS model via Mahmoud Ashraf's ctc-forced-aligner package (which saves a whole lot of headaches dealing with fairseq). To get multi-lingual coverage across many languages, the aligner operates on Romanised representations of the input text (derived via uroman). While it strips away punctuation internally, the returned output with word-level timestamps on the original script thankfully retains them.
